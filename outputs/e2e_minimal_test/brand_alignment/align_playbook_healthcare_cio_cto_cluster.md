## **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

### **The Inflection Point Healthcare Technology Leaders Face**

Healthcare technology leaders operate at a critical moment. They're managing unprecedented regulatory pressure while modernizing infrastructure under tight budget constraints. At the same time, 54% cite staffing shortages in AI, cybersecurity, and data science as a top hurdle.

This isn't a generic enterprise IT problem. Healthcare leaders face distinct pressures:

**Regulatory Complexity Is Intensifying**

January 2025 brought HHS proposals to "strengthen the cybersecurity of electronic protected health information"—changes that will reshape how health systems approach HIPAA compliance. The most significant revision eliminates the distinction between "required" and "addressable" controls, meaning every covered entity must now implement uniform security measures across the board.

**Your Attack Surface Just Expanded**

The 2024 Change Healthcare ransomware attack exposed the stakes. A $22 million ransom, over $2 billion in losses, and millions of patient records compromised. That wakeup call came as healthcare CISOs watched security budgets shrink—growing only 4% in 2024 versus 6% in 2023, while other industries grew 2+ percentage points more.

**Budget Growth Isn't Matching Scope**

Security budgets are flat. Regulatory obligations just expanded. Meanwhile, 67% of healthcare CIOs identify interoperability as a significant barrier to digital transformation.

## **WHO YOU'RE TALKING TO**

### **The Market**

The US healthcare IT market is $148.2 billion across 156,442 healthcare companies. That's 64% of global healthcare IT spend.

Post-pandemic, providers and payers increased IT investment to fuel innovation and improve operations. This year alone saw 14% growth—the fastest pace since Windows 95 and the early web.

Key priorities: IT infrastructure and cybersecurity to mitigate attack risk, clinical workflow optimization, data platforms, and interoperability.

**Regulatory Backdrop**

The HIPAA Security Rule is designed to be flexible, scalable, and technology-neutral. But the proposed 2025 updates mandate specific controls: multi-factor authentication, network segmentation, encryption of data at rest and in transit, and more rigorous vendor oversight.

### **The Role: CIO/CTO/CISO Decision Dynamics**

**The CIO's New Mandate**

CIOs aren't just IT operations managers anymore. They're business strategists working alongside C-level peers and boards to create business models for the modern era. They manage IT investment processes and frame questions about which opportunities actually work.

**Decision Authority Has Shifted**

Power moved from departmental users to C-suite executives and central stakeholders (CIOs, CEOs, government). This creates tension when consolidating vendors and reducing IT contracts.

**The Buying Committee Is Complex**

60-70% of organizations report five or more people involved in IT decisions. Over 25% say 10 or more, spanning executive leadership, clinicians, finance, operations, and beyond.

**Timelines Are Long**

50% of healthcare IT purchases take over 19 months. That's up from 12 months five years ago.

### **What Actually Drives Healthcare Technology Buying**

**#1 Selection Factor:** Proven success with other healthcare customers. Interoperability and security also top the list.

**#2 Priority:** ROI and cost justification. Organizations ask, "How can your solutions help us cut back on labor?" This links directly to the #1 priority: showing proven ability to deliver measurable outcomes.

**#3 Priority:** Integration and interoperability. Achieving true system integration remains elusive for most providers, even when the need is obvious.

**Budget Control**

Healthcare CIOs control 15-25% of total IT budgets in large health systems (500+ beds). Cybersecurity/infrastructure budgets are rising quickly—from a constrained baseline. Advisory services are purchased as part of strategic initiative budgets, not operational OPEX.
