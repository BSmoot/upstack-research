## **1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

This research examined how enterprise buyers discover, evaluate, and select technology vendors; what language resonates during that journey; and what creates skepticism around vendor-neutral advisory models.

### **Key Findings:**

Vendor selection is universally described by enterprise buyers as "daunting and frustrating," with challenges including poor user adoption, unmet expectations, analysis paralysis, and extended evaluation periods.



Research indicates that 52% of procurement professionals spend over 10 hours per week processing vendor-related tasks, and without clear evaluation frameworks, teams cycle through endless demos, RFPs, and stakeholder meetings without making meaningful progress, leading to extended evaluation periods, missed deadlines, and mounting frustration.

Vendor selection "can be one of the most time-consuming parts of the process" and "requires careful planning and execution if you want to avoid wasting time and money on bad vendors - but it's also critical to get the job done quickly."

**Primary buyer pain manifests as:**

Procurement and finance teams lose up to 27% of work-hours fixing supplier data issues and vendor-related problems, and poor supplier data costs the average company roughly $15 million per year.

When software vendor evaluation is inadequate, implementation timelines stretch and budgets balloon, with projects exceeding their planned scope by over 85%, and hidden costs compound over time, accumulating quarter after quarter, eroding the business case.

87% of procurement leaders have experienced being stuck with a supplier they do not wish to use, yet only 17% of companies have a robust procurement setup to actively prevent vendor lock-in.

**Critically, the research reveals:**

The difficulty of answering who has real purchasing power is a fundamental problem in tech sales to enterprise—there is usually a cast of dozens or hundreds of people to satisfy, and their needs don't always overlap.



According to Forrester's 2024 Buyers' Journey Survey, the average purchase decision involves 13 stakeholders.

**Language that works:**

Successful technology advisors and brokers use problem-centric, not solution-centric language.

Vendor-agnostic advisors provide "unbiased advice centered on your specific needs," and "free from the pressure of quotas, they recommend solutions solely based on what benefits your business."



Working with a Technology Advisor "is like asking a server for advice at a restaurant"—positioning the advisor as a guide through overwhelming choices.

**Language that confuses:**

Technology advisors describe themselves with many different words: consultant, solution provider, trusted advisor, broker—but these terms are customer-centric and don't clearly differentiate across models.

 Generic terms like "vendor-neutral" and "trusted advisor" trigger skepticism without proof.

Based on research into B2B buying committee dynamics and procurement skepticism:

#### **OBJECTION 1: "Why not just go direct to vendors? We get better pricing than brokers usually offer."**

**Research Context:**

IT professionals often question: "I get Net30 terms from Dell and HP directly—why pay some guy to send the email and place an order for you?"

**THE MISTAKE:** Defending the commission model. Instead, reframe the real advantage.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**

*"That makes sense for straightforward purchases. But here's what direct sales misses:*

- *Vendors will quote you their standard terms. No negotiation.*

When procurement leaders ask for aggressive discounts, salespeople get frustrated and say they can't meet those requests. It takes education to tell them why they actually can—vendors have 70-80% margins, so discounts are possible if you know to ask.

- *You're comparing one vendor. We show you three with scored recommendations based on your actual needs—not what the sales team wants to sell you.*

- *Going direct means your IT team spends weeks on vendor calls. We handle that.*

52% of procurement professionals spend over 10 hours per week on vendor-related tasks, cycling through endless demos and RFPs without meaningful progress.

*The math: You get the same pricing (or better), faster decisions, and your team focuses on what they're paid for—managing your technology, not managing vendor relationships."*

**SUPPORTING PROOF:**

Brokers "often have established relationships with vendors, which allows them to negotiate better pricing and terms on your behalf. When companies go it alone, they're less likely to secure favorable deals, especially if they lack the buying power or knowledge to leverage during negotiations."

#### **OBJECTION 2: "How do I know you're neutral if vendors are paying you?"**

**Research Context:**

Salespeople "preempt objections before they reach the CFO" by framing their solution as "the lowest-risk, highest-upside option." Buyers want proof of impartiality.

**THE MISTAKE:** Saying "trust me" or claiming "performance-based" (dishonest). Instead, explain the *structural* mechanism that enforces neutrality.

**RESPONSE SCRIPT:**

*"This is the most important question. Here's why the model enforces neutrality, not undermines it:*

*1. We only get paid when you're satisfied.*

Vendor sales reps are driven by quotas and commission-based goals, which creates bias. A vendor-agnostic advisor provides unbiased advice centered on your needs, free from quota pressure, recommending solutions based on what benefits your business.

*2. We stay with you after the contract. If you bought the wrong vendor, you'll replace them—and we lose the commission. That's aligned with your success, not a transaction.*

*3. We evaluate all 300+ vendors in your space, not our 'preferred partners.' Our 322 active supplier relationships give us real market intelligence most advisors lack.*

*4. Check our 78 NPS score and 99.8% retention rate. If we were recommending the wrong vendors, clients wouldn't stay.*

*You can also ask for references from our clients. They'll tell you whether we recommended solutions that worked or vendors that failed."*

**SUPPORTING PROOF:**

With a technology broker model, "you'll typically receive the same pricing—or even better—compared to going directly to the provider. Brokers are compensated by the vendors you choose, ensuring their motivation aligns with finding and maintaining the best service for your needs."

Technology advisors "leverage their industry relationships to identify the best deals" and "have established trust and rapport with providers, allowing them to secure favorable terms and conditions that are otherwise challenging for individual businesses to achieve."
