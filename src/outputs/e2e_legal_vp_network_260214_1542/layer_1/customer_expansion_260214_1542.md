## 3. CROSS-SELL & UPSELL PATTERNS

### Service Combination Patterns

Upselling: refers to selling additional benefits and features to a customer on top of a product or service they've already purchased. Examples could be things like extra storage space, one-on-one support time, or additional admin seats for team members. Cross-selling: refers to selling complementary, standalone products or services suitable for the same customer's needs. Examples might be a related software package or affiliate products or services from a partner brand.

For advisory services specifically, common expansions follow these patterns:

| Initial Service | Expansion #1 | Expansion #2 | Expansion #3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Network evaluation | Security assessment | Cloud advisory | IT modernization |
| Vendor negotiation (1 category) | 2nd category consolidation | Portfolio-wide vendor review | Ongoing lifecycle mgmt |
| Single vendor selection | Contract terms review | Multi-year relationship mgmt | Technology roadmapping |

### Scope Expansion Mechanics

There are two main ways that you can drive expansion within an account. We know them as upsells—offering an upgraded version of the product—and cross-sells—offering new products/features that will subsequently provide an upgraded stack of the product. Both of these can happen during the customer's mid-life term, or during the renewal process.

**How scopes expand:**
- Horizontal: Adding new technology categories (networking → security → infrastructure)
- Vertical: Deepening engagement (selection → negotiation → ongoing management)
- Temporal: Moving from one-time engagements to recurring advisory

## 4. CUSTOMER LIFETIME VALUE PROGRESSION

### LTV Trajectory for Advisory Relationships

Suppose that a given client is generating an average of $20,000 annually in gross revenue. Now suppose that on average, you retain a client for 15 years. Over the client's lifetime of association with your firm, the client will contribute a total of $300,000 in revenues. If you know that your annual costs for serving that client are $14,000, then the client's CLV looks like this: ($20,000 X 15) – ($14,000 X 15) OR $300,000 – $210,000 = $90,000.

### Predictors of High vs. Low LTV

**High-LTV profiles** demonstrate:

Firms that run regular QBRs report 33% higher expansion revenue and a lower likelihood of silent churn

- Multi-vendor environments (more consolidation opportunity)
- Annual technology budgets exceeding $10M
- Formal procurement processes with regular renewal cycles
- Decentralized IT with multiple stakeholders

**Low-LTV profiles** tend to:
- Have monolithic vendor relationships
- Make infrequent technology decisions
- Operate with tight IT budgets
- Have high staff turnover in procurement

### Revenue Growth Patterns

Net dollar retention (NDR) measures the percentage of recurring revenue retained from existing customers over a period, after accounting for expansions, downgrades and churn. It is a vital indicator of customer satisfaction and growth. A high NDR suggests that customers stay and increase their spending, through upsells or additional purchases.

**Healthy advisory expansion shows:**
- Year 1: Base engagement value
- Year 2: 20-40% expansion (new categories, deeper scope)
- Year 3+: 10-25% annual growth (ongoing advisory, relationship deepening)
