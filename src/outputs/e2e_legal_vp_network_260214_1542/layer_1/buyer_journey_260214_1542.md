## **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: 7 Key Findings**

1. **Reliability Crisis Drives Hidden Costs** –

The average cost of a single hour of downtime now exceeds $300,000 for over 90% of mid-size and large enterprises, with

four in 10 enterprises indicating that hourly downtime costs their firms $1 million to over $5 million. This makes reliability the paramount decision criterion—not price.

2. **Procurement Process Lacks Authority Clarity** –

Modern enterprise buying committees have 3.1 to 4.6 distinct groups inside companies that shape the average purchase process, creating consensus-driven delays.

Only 15% of IT procurement decisions were made by large committees, with purchases most frequently made either by one to two people, or in a consensus scenario, involving no more than five individuals.

3. **Discovery Happens Before Vendors Engage** –

41% of B2B buyers already have a preferred vendor before formal evaluation even begins, and

83% of technology buyers report mostly or fully defining their purchase requirements before speaking with sales. This means advisor value occurs in the **pre-RFP phase**.

4. **SD-WAN Adoption Accelerating; Evaluation Bottlenecks Persist** –

Around 66% of Fortune 1000 companies have active SD-WAN projects, yet

simple software purchases can be completed in a few days, while complex implementations, such as enterprise systems or security solutions, may take three to six months.

5. **Cost Optimization Drives Procurement Decisions** –

Cost optimization is again front and center, pushing CIOs and procurement leaders to prioritize solutions that help identify, capture, and measure savings opportunities, with increased scrutiny on software vendor selection and stronger alignment between IT sourcing and financial outcomes.

6. **Advisory Model is Invisible to Buyers** – Buyers commonly confuse vendor-neutral advisors with MSPs, VARs, and consultants.

Tech advisors function more in the actual procurement of technology, yet

many technology consulting services are provided at no cost to businesses because the technology service providers typically pay for the consultancy services, with service providers understanding that these free consultancy services can assist businesses in selecting the right technology solutions.

7. **Trust Skepticism Remains High** –

The stakes are higher—if a buyer makes the wrong decision, they can lose their job; this need to protect their career shapes how they decide. Enterprise buyers remain cautious about perceived advisor independence when vendors pay advisors.

## **BUYER JOURNEY MAP**

### Stage 1: **TRIGGER → PROBLEM RECOGNITION** (Weeks -4 to 0)
- **Activities:** Internal impact assessment; business case documentation; stakeholder alignment on pain points
- **Decision-Makers Involved:** CIO, Network Director, Finance VP, Operations Manager
- **Information Needs:** Cost-of-downtime calculations; business impact analysis; current SLA compliance metrics
- **Timeline:** 2-4 weeks
- **Advisory Fit:** **HIGH** – Advisors can quantify hidden costs and frame the business case BEFORE formal procurement begins

### Stage 2: **DISCOVERY → SOLUTION RESEARCH** (Weeks 0-4)
- **Activities:** Peer research; analyst report reviews (Gartner, TrustRadius); self-directed vendor research
- **Decision-Makers Involved:** IT practitioners; technical evaluators; procurement team members
- **Information Sources:**

Public product review sites are now the most consulted information source (31%) for software buying, with independent peer feedback outweighing vendor narratives and analyst coverage at every stage

- **Timing:** This occurs BEFORE vendor engagement (~41% come with preferred vendors already selected)
- **Advisory Fit:** **CRITICAL** – This is where vendor-neutral advisors add maximum value: pre-preference shortlisting, market intelligence

### Stage 3: **EVALUATION → RFP/DEMO** (Weeks 4-12)
- **Activities:** Formal RFP issuance; vendor demos; technical due diligence; reference calls
- **Decision-Makers Involved:** Cross-functional committee (IT, Finance, Security, Legal, Operations)
- **Evaluation Criteria:**

Successful SD-WAN procurement requires evaluating both technical capabilities and cost structures, creating detailed requirements matrices organized by site tier and criticality levels, and requesting component-level pricing breakdowns

- **Timeline:** 6-8 weeks typical for SD-WAN; 3-6 months for enterprise solutions
- **Advisory Fit:** **MODERATE-HIGH** – Helps navigate vendor responses; manages RFP logistics; tests vendor claims against market reality

### Stage 4: **DECISION → SELECTION & NEGOTIATION** (Weeks 12-16)
- **Activities:** Vendor scoring; contract negotiation; final stakeholder approval
- **Decision-Makers Involved:** Executive sponsor (CFO/CIO); procurement; legal
- **Friction Points:**

Lengthy approval cycles may become drawn out due to complex approval hierarchies, reliance on manual processes, poor interdepartmental communication, and limited visibility into progress

- **Timeline:** 4-6 weeks
- **Advisory Fit:** **HIGH** – Negotiation leverage; contract term optimization; vendor risk assessment

### Stage 5: **IMPLEMENTATION → GO-LIVE** (Weeks 16+)
- **Engagement Model Changes:** Advisor transitions from "buyer advocate" to "implementation oversight" and "vendor SLA monitoring"
- **Timeline:**

Simple software purchases can be completed in a few days, while complex implementations, such as enterprise systems or security solutions, may take three to six months

- **Advisory Fit:** **VERY HIGH** – Ongoing vendor accountability; performance monitoring; renewal planning
