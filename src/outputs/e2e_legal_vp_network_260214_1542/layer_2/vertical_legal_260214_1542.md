## VERTICAL REGULATORY & COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS

### A. Core Regulatory Framework

The American Bar Association (ABA) Model Rules of Professional Conduct set the ethical standards for attorneys across the United States. These rules serve as the foundation for legal ethics and have been adopted (with modifications) by most U.S. states.

**Key Rules Affecting Infrastructure:**

ABA Model Rule 1.6(c) requires lawyers to "make reasonable efforts to prevent the inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure of, or unauthorized access to, information relating to the representation of a client."

Rule 5.3 (Responsibilities Regarding Nonlawyer Assistance) requires law firms to ensure third-party vendors, including IT providers, comply with ethical obligations. Rule 1.15 (Safeguarding Client Property) requires lawyers to protect client funds and data against cybersecurity threats. These rules establish a duty for attorneys to maintain technological competence and protect client confidentiality—which means strong IT security measures must be in place.

### B. Data Privacy & Security Regulations

The CCPA strives to mirror the GDPR and requires enhanced protection of personal data for California residents. In 2023, an amendment to the CCPA, Proposition 24, the CPRA, came into effect.

GDPR, a unified EU data protection law since 2018, emphasizes enhanced personal data protection. It's relevant globally, with many states adopting similar statutes.

HIPAA: The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) is a federal law that requires healthcare providers and "business associates" to protect protected health information (PHI) from inadvertent disclosure. Since law firms are considered business associates, they must comply with HIPAA when handling PHI on behalf of their clients.

As of April 2025, 21 US states have passed comprehensive consumer data privacy laws, though Nevada's law is older, and, like Florida's, generally considered less comprehensive. California was the first state to pass a modern and comprehensive regulation, and many others have followed, creating a complex regulatory landscape for businesses operating across multiple states.

### C. Consequences of Non-Compliance

Cybersecurity is now a core component of ethical legal practice. Lawyers must move beyond traditional methods of protecting client confidentiality and proactively implement robust cybersecurity measures to fulfill their professional obligations. Doing so not only ensures compliance with ABA Model Rule 1.6(c) but also preserves client trust and upholds the integrity of the legal profession.

Law firms are increasingly obtaining cyber insurance to transfer some of the risks to confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data in their computers and information systems. This emerging form of insurance can cover gaps in more traditional forms of insurance, covering areas like restoration of data, incident response costs, and liability for data breaches.
