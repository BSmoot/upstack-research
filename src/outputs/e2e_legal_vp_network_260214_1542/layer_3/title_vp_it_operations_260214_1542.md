## **TITLE ROLE PROFILE & EVOLUTION**

### **IT Manager (Entry/Mid-Level)**

At the entry level, IT Operations Managers are primarily engaged in the day-to-day maintenance of IT systems and responding to technical issues. Their daily activities often include monitoring system performance, troubleshooting, and working closely with technical support teams.

Mid-level IT Operations Managers take on a more strategic role within the IT department, focusing on process optimization and team leadership. They are responsible for managing a subset of the IT operations team and working on projects that improve operational efficiency.

### **Director of IT Operations**

Directors of IT Operations have a broad scope of responsibility, including budgeting, defining operational procedures, and leading large-scale initiatives.

They are involved in strategic planning and may contribute to policy development, disaster recovery planning, and high-level decision-making.

### **VP IT Operations**

Senior IT Operations Managers often have strategic responsibilities, making decisions that affect the entire IT landscape of an organization and potentially influencing business-wide strategies.

A VP of IT is responsible for leading IT strategy, overseeing infrastructure, managing IT teams, ensuring system security, and aligning IT with business goals. They also handle budgeting, vendor relationships, technology implementations, and risk management.

### **Evolution of the Role (Recent Changes)**

Today IT operations managers can still see a lot, but they can touch very little. While some companies still own a significant amount of hardware, a diminishing number are making new hardware purchases. They're moving toward Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) solutions. The role of the modern IT operations manager is to make sure that people can get at the information they need, when they need it, in a manner that is easy, appropriate and secure.

Now, procurement is about getting the best hourly rate for a given service. It's a different way of doing business.

Apply a scoring model that mirrors risk and impact. Security/compliance and integration fit often carry the heaviest weights, followed by reliability/SLAs and TCO. Publish the weights so stakeholders understand trade-offs before the vendor evaluation process begins.

Technology infrastructure vendors should be assessed for reliability, scalability, and security. For managed services, evaluate response times, service-level agreements (SLAs).

The vendor evaluation process helps you identify vulnerabilities before starting the project. You can easily verify information, such as their qualifications and certifications, track performance, and even look into the supplier's financials.

## **OBJECTIONS & CONCERNS**

### **Common Objections to Advisors**

**"We already have a procurement team."**
- They handle contracts, not strategy or technology evaluation. Advisory adds domain expertise.

**"We don't have budget for consulting."**
- Advisory fee is commission-based (vendor-funded). Savings pay for the engagement.

**"We're tied into long-term contracts; there's no flexibility."**

Start by negotiating shorter initial contract durations – aim for one to two years instead of locking yourself into a three- to five-year commitment. Shorter contracts give you more opportunities to renegotiate or switch providers without steep penalties.

**"Changing vendors will disrupt operations."**

Having to simultaneously learn a new tool, transfer information onto it from the old tool and stay on top of a normal workload can be nerve-wracking, so leave as little uncertainty as possible. As you progress through your plans and the implementation process, continue to communicate about what's going on. Provide continuous updates and reminders so the new software adoption doesn't get forgotten by anyone.

### **What Causes Skepticism About Advisors**

Management teams and operating partners are resistant to change their embedded ways and processes. And sometimes, tech adoption is stymied by those who perceive the procurement and implementation to be an unwanted headache.

**Past Experience**:

If they've invested in technology in the past and never really used it, any technology implementation must be accompanied by a change management plan, one that has executive buy-in, drives adoption, and incentivizes use across the ecosystem.

### **Concerns About Changing Vendors**

1. **Data migration risk** –

Since the cloud is still new, standards of ownership, i.e. who actually owns the data once it lives on a cloud platform, are not yet developed, which could make it complicated if cloud computing users ever decide to move data off of a cloud vendor's platform.

2. **Hidden switching costs** –

High switching costs emerge from investments in training, customization, and integration that would need to be replicated with a new vendor. Long-term contracts often include financial penalties for early termination.

3. **Integration complexity** –

When EHS software fails to integrate with your existing tools, you're left with tough choices: either replace multiple systems to align with the new platform or accept reduced functionality and rely on manual workarounds. Both options increase complexity and add to operational costs. Changing regulations can also force additional spending on new modules if the system doesn't link with updated compliance tools.
