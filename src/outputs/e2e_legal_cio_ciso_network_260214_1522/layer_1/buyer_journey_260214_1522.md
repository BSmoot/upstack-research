## **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: 7 KEY FINDINGS**

1. **Buyer Remorse Crisis Is Acute**:

80% of tech buyers experience some form of post-purchase regret, with

60% of technology buyers involved in renewal decisions regretting nearly every purchase they make.

Conflict within buying teams is a more significant driver of regret than product quality.

2. **Buying Groups Are Large, Fragmented, and Increasingly Risk-Averse**:

On average, 13 people within an organization are involved in the buying decision, with 89% of purchases involving two or more departments.

Buyers are expanding buying groups to reduce risk, involving procurement earlier and insisting on trials to validate value before committing.

3. **Evaluation Processes Are Lengthy, Resource-Intensive, and Plagued by Stalling**:

86% of B2B purchases stall during the buying process and 81% of buyers express dissatisfaction with their chosen providers.

Technology purchasing decisions take three to six months—or more—of deliberation, and 90% of IT Executives in North America see software sourcing and vendor selection as a pain point.

4. **AI Is Rapidly Reshaping Discovery and Evaluation but Creating Trust Tensions**:

89% of buyers in Forrester's Buyers' Journey Survey, 2024, reported that they were using genAI in at least one area of their purchasing process, yet

buyers embrace generative AI for research but do not trust it enough to rely on it alone.

70% of B2B software buyers now use AI search tools, and one in four begin their search in an AI chatbot.

5. **Information Sources Are Democratizing; Peer and Community Voices Now Compete with Analysts**:

Crowd-sourced peer-reviewed websites now account for a huge part of the research process, with sites like Capterra, G2 Crowd, and TrustRadius having millions of visitors each month and hundreds of reviews on dozens of products.

Analysts like Gartner, Forrester, and their ilk remain influential, but they rarely give a full picture of the market and rarely move at the speed of business.

6. **Cost and Hidden Expenses Are Systemic Sources of Dissatisfaction**:

Although a technology option might appear reasonable at first, the cost of regular upkeep, upgrades, hidden costs and training can quickly add up, causing tech buyers who don't consider TCO before making a purchase to frequently end up regretting their investment.

Software is no longer a set-it-and-forget-it purchase, and bait-and-switch pricing is common in the software space, with vendors burying important costs in their terms and conditions.

7. **Vendor-Neutral Advisory Has Minimal Awareness; Buyers Confuse It with Consultants, Analysts, and Direct Vendors**: Research did not identify evidence that "vendor-neutral advisory" exists as a recognized category in enterprise buyer consciousness. Buyers default to analyst firms, peer reviews, internal IT teams, or direct vendor engagement.

Advisory models addressing vendor proliferation, feature overlap, pricing complexity, and integration challenges position themselves as objective guides helping organizations make informed decisions that align technology investments with business outcomes, but this category remains nascent and largely invisible.

## **KEY DECISION-MAKING FRICTION POINTS**

### **What Causes Evaluation Processes to Stall**

1. **Lack of Consensus Among Decision-Makers** (56% of high-regret cases)
   -

Disagreement over key objectives and a tendency to vacillate, stall and face objections by occasional decision makers led to buyer remorse in 56% of purchases; most of the buyer groups in the Gartner study consisted of eight to 15 people, and two-thirds of those involved in the decision process were not in IT.
   - **Remedies:** Clear executive sponsorship, explicit decision-making authority, aligned KPIs.

2. **Over-Specification & Requirement Creep**
   -

Many organizations get the enterprise software selection process right until the RFI/RFP moment, because many still use outdated RFP platforms which are riddled with bias and exponentially slow down the process.
   - **Remedies:** Disciplined requirements gathering, clear must-haves vs. nice-to-haves, weighted scoring from the start.

3. **Insufficient Internal Stakeholder Alignment on Business Outcomes**
   -

Engaging and aligning the right stakeholders for requirements-gathering drives digital transformation and results in high user adoption; valuing the right voices creates a compounding benefit throughout the software buying process.
   - **Remedies:** Cross-functional workshops early; business case with explicit success metrics.

4. **Procurement & Legal Delays**
   - Contract negotiations, security assessments, and vendor financial due diligence often become bottlenecks.
   - **Remedies:** Pre-negotiate template terms; parallel legal review during evaluation.

5. **POC/Pilot Fatigue**
   -

The likelihood of engaging in a trial increases with the size and cost of the purchase; technology decision-makers are particularly likely to rely on trials to evaluate how solutions will work in real-world conditions; however, trials do not guarantee loyalty, with 36% of buyers planning to convert to a fully paid version from the same provider after a trial, 35% planning to convert with a different provider, and 10% planning to begin a new trial of the same solution with a different vendor.
   - **Remedies:** Define POC success criteria upfront; time-box pilots; use structured evaluation frameworks to decide between options post-POC.
