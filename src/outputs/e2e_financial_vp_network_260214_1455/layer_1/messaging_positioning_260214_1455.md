## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: KEY FINDINGS

### Primary Research Insights

1. **Buyer Language Signals Procedural Mistrust Over Cost Concern**


Buyers fear "vendors taking control of the process and injecting serious bias" into vendor selection, indicating the core pain is loss of objectivity, not simply cost reduction. This reframes the value proposition away from "free" toward "unbiased."

2. **Enterprise Procurement is Fragmented and Chaotic**


Most organizations struggle with procurement because they don't have a clear, repeatable process, remaining stuck in a reactive loop of lost requests, unreviewed contracts, security failures, and overspending on tools. The category opportunity sits at the intersection of structure + objectivity.

3. **Commission-Based Models Work When Trust Mechanics Are Right**


Commission-based advisors carry inherent bias concerns because they earn money through products sold, but

fiduciary duty and legal obligation to act in clients' best interests generally builds trustworthiness. Long-term relationship incentive structure is critical proof point.

4. **Cost Savings Metrics That Resonate**


Cost savings is calculated as: Initial proposed cost - actual cost = cost savings (e.g., traditional spend of $10,000 reduced to $7,000 = $3,000 savings or 30% savings percentage). Buyers want percentages and concrete baseline comparisons, not raw dollars.

5. **Time Compression Is Underestimated Proof Point**


Top procurement performers achieve 5-hour requisition-to-order cycle times; poor performers take 48 hours—more than 8x longer. Speed is a differentiator that buyers don't initially demand but quickly value when experienced.

6. **IT Buyers Live with Vendor Fatigue and Distrust**


In r/sysadmin forums, system administrators share honest assessments of enterprise software, reveal hidden costs of major implementations, and discuss real-world performance beyond marketing claims—this insider knowledge proves invaluable when making purchasing decisions.

7. **Procurement KPIs Signal What Matters**


Key procurement KPIs include: contract compliance rates, cost per invoice, spend under management, invoice accuracy, procurement cycle time, number of suppliers, and supplier availability. These are the metrics UPSTACK should be optimizing against in proof narratives.

## OBJECTION HANDLING SCRIPTS

### **Objection 1: "How do you stay neutral if vendors pay you?"**

**Root Cause:** Commission-based models inherently trigger conflict-of-interest concerns

**Script:**
"I understand that concern—it's legitimate. Here's how we've solved it: [1] Like financial advisors, our advisory is vendor-funded, not buyer-funded. You pay nothing. [2] We're incentivized differently than transactional brokers—we only earn ongoing compensation if you stay on your solution long-term. That means our incentive is your long-term satisfaction, not a one-time sale. [3] Our 99.8% retention rate proves this model produces accountability. If we were recommending poorly, clients wouldn't stay. [4] We recommend across our entire portfolio—not just the highest-paying vendors. In fact, sometimes our best recommendation is to stay with your current vendor rather than switching."

**Supporting Evidence:**

Fiduciary duty and legal obligation to act in clients' best interests generally builds trustworthiness

Trustworthiness plummets when you prioritize your interests over others'; self-orientation stands alone as the most powerful variable

- Long-term relationship + ongoing compensation = aligned incentive

**What NOT to Say:**
- "We're not commission-based" (false; undercuts credibility)
- "We don't have conflicts of interest" (everyone does)
- "We only recommend best-fit" (vague; unsubstantiated)

---

### **Objection 2: "Why not just go direct to vendors?"**

**Root Cause:** Buyers perceive vendors as cheaper / faster / simpler than involving an intermediary

**Script:**
"That's a fair question. Going direct works if you have three things: [1] Deep market knowledge of every supplier option in your category—who's actually competitive, who's inflated, who's on the rise or decline. [2] Negotiating leverage from thousands of comparable deals—what discounts are realistic, what terms are non-negotiable. [3] Time to run a rigorous evaluation process. Most vendors will show you their best features; ours is to help you compare against your actual needs. In practice, direct vendors spend 60-80% of the sales cycle selling features rather than uncovering your constraints. We flip that—we go deep on your problem first, then recommend. That's why our average evaluation takes 33 days instead of 6+ months."

**Supporting Evidence:**

Vendors take control of the process and inject bias—selling you things the business doesn't need

Demos need specific outcomes focused on business challenges, not just features

5-hour vs. 48-hour procurement cycles show that structure matters more than direct access

**What NOT to Say:**
- "Vendors are biased" (obvious; doesn't help)
- "We save you money" (unproven without context)
- "We provide transparency" (everyone claims this)

---

### **Objection 3: "We have internal IT—why do we need you?"**

**Root Cause:** IT teams see advisory as replacement threat; misunderstand the "adjacency, not replacement" model

**Script:**
"Exactly the right question. We're not here to replace your IT team—we're here to augment them. Your team is great at implementing and supporting technology. They're usually overloaded managing 30+ vendor relationships while also handling daily operations. Here's where we come in: [1] We handle vendor evaluation and selection—the research-heavy work that pulls your team away from strategy. [2] We negotiate contracts and manage the vendor lifecycle—renewals, escalations, performance reviews. [3] We stay as your single point of contact for vendor-related issues, freeing your team to focus on ops and innovation. Think of it as extending your team's capacity, not replacing it. We work *with* your IT leaders, not around them."

**Supporting Evidence:**

Workforce and talent shortages mean ITDMs worry if they have the right people to implement and maintain new technologies

The difficulty in tech sales is that there are usually dozens or hundreds of people to satisfy, and their needs don't always overlap

Strategic outsourcing allows advisors to focus on relationship-building and understanding client needs, achieving long-term loyalty and referrals

**What NOT to Say:**
- "You don't have time for vendor management" (sounds insulting)
- "We'll handle the boring vendor stuff" (diminishes IT's expertise)
- "Your team isn't vendor-savvy" (triggers defensiveness)

---

### **Objection 4: "We've used brokers before and had bad experiences"**

**Root Cause:** Prior experience with transactional brokers (commission-chasing, poor service, overselling) creates category skepticism

**Script:**
"I hear you. That's a common experience with traditional brokers. Here's what's different: Traditional brokers are paid per deal—they make money on transactions, not outcomes. So their incentive is to close fast, not solve right. We operate on a fundamentally different model. [1] We're compensated ongoing as your trusted advisor, not per-deal. [2] Our 3.5-year average client tenure means we stay through implementation, renewals, and ongoing management. [3] We invest in understanding your business deeply—it's not a speed-to-close game. [4] Our 99.8% retention proves clients come back for the second, third, and fourth evaluation because they got value the first time. Traditional brokers would have lost half those clients by year two. We're built for long-term value, not transaction velocity."

**Supporting Evidence:**

The economics of repeat business are massively lower costs and higher value for both parties; relationships are built on longer-term commitments, not one-off transactions; trust is at the heart

Agents must hold the line to protect how they make a living; firm confident responses evidence that an agent believes in services performed; failure to stand firm early increases likelihood of fending off additional attempts later

**What NOT to Say:**
- "We're not like those brokers" (empty claim without proof)
- "We're different because we care" (unmeasurable)
- "We provide better service" (everyone says this)

**Supporting Evidence:**

**What NOT to Say:**
- "It's free—vendors pay us" (sounds too good to be true without context)
