### **Finding 1: Downtime Costs Drive Urgency Beyond Enterprise Procurement Conversations**

Large enterprises face staggering downtime costs—the automotive sector incurs $2.3 million per hour, while heavy industry faces $59 million annually from unplanned outages.

100% of surveyed executives reported experiencing outage-related revenue losses in the past year, with per-outage losses ranging from at least $10,000 to over $1,000,000. This creates a **dominant trigger event** that transcends typical budget-cycle procurement—network failures become existential business risks that compel action.

### **Finding 2: AI is Transforming the Evaluation Phase—But Not in Ways Most Expect**

94% of buyers use LLMs during their buying process and 89% ultimately purchase solutions with AI features. However,

AI makes buyers feel informed, but buying networks make decisions harder to control.

Buyers arrive over-confident, buying networks shape decisions, and shortlists form early. For network vendors, this means the window to influence evaluation has compressed—buyers form AI-informed opinions before meaningful vendor engagement.

### **Finding 3: Peer Recommendations Now Outweigh Vendor Direct Contact by 1.4x**

B2B buyers report that they value third-party interactions 1.4x more than digital supplier interactions.

73% of B2B marketing executives rank word-of-mouth and peer recommendations as the most influential factor in deciding which vendors to consider, and 56% of buyers consult with existing product users before purchasing. For network solutions, **peer validation has become the primary decision mechanism**—vendor roadmaps and feature comparisons are secondary.

### **Finding 4: Vendor-Neutral Advisory Exists but Remains Largely Invisible to Enterprise Buyers**
Our research surface

d evidence of several vendor-neutral technology advisory models in the market—including

Technology Services Distributors (TSDs), or tech services brokerages, that hold contracts with suppliers and pass between 70% and 95% of commissions to subagent advisors. However,

Technology advisors represent one of the fastest growing indirect sales channels for vendors and customers, but the world is still learning exactly what they do. Most enterprises don't distinguish advisor models from traditional consulting, VARs, or MSPs.

### **Finding 5: The Decision Timeline Compresses When Downtime Risk is Present**

The tech buyer's journey has three main stages: Awareness and discovery (problem identification), Consideration and research (exploring solutions), and Engagement and decision making (vendor selection). However, when network availability becomes crisis-driven, this timeline collapses. Infrastructure refresh cycles are typically **3-7 years**, but triggered by

end-of-life dates—if a device goes EOL within 18 months, budget replacement now, and don't wait for a failure during a critical project.

### **Finding 6: CIOs Prefer Non-Vendor Information Sources Early; Vendor Engagement Peaks Late**

Early in the decision process, most CIOs prefer information sources other than the vendor, including analyst firms like Forrester. When IT professionals are buying a technology solution, they rely on touchpoints outside of the vendor's control, including industry publications and websites, forums or message boards and conversations with peers.

CIO engagement is highest in the final phases of the buying process, when vendors have the most sway in purchasing decisions.

### **Finding 7: Advisory Model Trust Barrier is Skepticism About Vendor Funding, Not Service Value**
Organizations understand that

advisors provide their services to clients at no cost, earning fees from vendors through channel programs. These fees do not impact the client's deal discount. However, many enterprises are unaware this model exists. When informed, the primary objection is **not** the vendor-funded model itself, but rather whether the advisor's independence is genuine—a barrier that UPSTACK's 99.8% retention rate and transparent disclosure model directly addresses.
