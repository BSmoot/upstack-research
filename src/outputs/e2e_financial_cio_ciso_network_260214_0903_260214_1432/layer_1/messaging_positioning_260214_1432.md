## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: KEY INSIGHTS

### The Problem Buyers Actually Articulate

From vendor-neutral advisory discussions to IT forums, enterprise buyers consistently describe their technology procurement challenges using these language patterns:

Vendor selection can be daunting and frustrating, with many organizations facing challenges such as poor user adoption, unmet expectations, and missed stakeholder requirements.

 More specifically,

organizations lack a vendor selection criteria checklist and struggle to compare options objectively, which leads to extended evaluation periods, missed deadlines, and mounting frustration across IT, procurement, and business stakeholders.

### What Creates Skepticism

Buyers want the vendor-neutral perspective when figuring out their tech challenges.

 However, the unfamiliar commission-based model creates immediate resistance.

When advisors are influenced by vendor incentives, businesses risk being sold solutions that may not align with their goals.

 Trust must be earned through demonstrating that the model is executed in ways that align buyer and advisor interests.

### Why The "Free" Message Fails

When vendors offer "free advisory," buyers hear "someone else is paying them to recommend something"—not "you get expert guidance at no cost." The commission model requires reframing around **accountability** and **outcomes**, not cost elimination.

## 3. OBJECTION HANDLING SCRIPTS

### Objection #1: "Why not go direct to vendors?"

**Root Fear:** "We'll get worse pricing/terms than if we negotiate ourselves."

**Researched Response (with proof):**

*Initial Reframe:*

Procurement consultants have access to market data, familiarity with suppliers and their commercial models, and negotiation tactics. A business's purchases are on an entirely different scale compared to retail customers, so the buyer needs to research more than just price; vendor reliability, reputation, and timeliness of delivery can be just as critical as the bottom line.

*Specific Counter:*
- **"You negotiate one vendor every 2-3 years. We negotiate 300+ per year."** That's the gap.
- **"Vendors give different pricing based on who's asking. We know what they're offering your competitor down the street."**

In SaaS, margins reach 70-80%, meaning the discount opportunity is also 70-80%. Most IT leaders ask for 10-15% discounts because they don't understand vendor economics.

**Proof to Deploy:**

15–30% cost savings within 6–12 months through spend analytics & supplier consolidation

- Case study: Customer saved 28% on network renewal through UPSTACK's market intelligence vs. their direct negotiation

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ "We get better pricing because vendors pay us commission" (triggers conflict assumption)
- ❌ "You should trust us instead of negotiating yourself" (defensive)

---

### Objection #2: "How do you stay neutral if vendors pay you?"

**Root Fear:** "You'll recommend the expensive vendor because they pay more commission."

**Researched Response:**

*Structural Proof:*

Advisors provide their services to clients at no cost, earning fees from vendors through channel programs. These fees do not impact the client's deal discount.

*Accountability Model:*
- **"We're only paid when you renew. That means our incentive is your long-term satisfaction, not the deal size."**
- **"If we recommend wrong, you switch. We lose a 3.5-year customer relationship."** (UPSTACK's average tenure)
- **"Our 99.8% retention proves the model produces accountability."**

*Analogy That Works:*

"This makes the relationship more like that of fixed fee financial advisor."

 — Buyers understand fiduciary-adjacent relationships better than commission-based ones.

**Proof to Deploy:**
- 99.8% retention rate (implies clients stay only if satisfied)
- 78 NPS (industry-leading; proves recommendations are sound)

5,399 active revenue-generating client relationships with $55M+ in total MRR under management

 (scale = diverse customer base, not cherry-picked)

**What NOT to say:**
- ❌ "We don't earn commissions" (lie)
- ❌ "We're performance-based, not commission-based" (semantic dodge; buyers see through it)
