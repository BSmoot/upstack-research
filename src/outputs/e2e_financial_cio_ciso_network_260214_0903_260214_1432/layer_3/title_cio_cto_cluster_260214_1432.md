## ROLE DEFINITION & RESPONSIBILITIES

### CIO: Chief Information Officer

**Primary Mandate:**

The CIO's job is to innovate, collaborate, balance the IT budget and motivate IT staff.

A CIO is a high-ranking executive responsible for managing and implementing technology systems within an organization, with their main responsibility being to streamline and optimize those systems to meet business goals.

**Core Responsibilities (2026 Definition):**

Strategic IT leadership includes developing and implementing long-term IT strategies aligned with business objectives. Infrastructure management involves overseeing and optimizing an organization's entire infrastructure including hardware, software, networks, and data centers. IT governance and security includes creating policies to improve cybersecurity and compliance and mitigate risks. Resource optimization involves managing IT department budgets and approving new technology investments.

**Evolution of the Role:**

The CIO is no longer evaluated on systems delivered, but on how effectively technology improves productivity, resilience and decision-making.

In 2026 the CIO role is about orchestrating technology in a way that enables speed without increasing risk.

**Three Critical Deliverables for 2026:**

Business alignment "ensuring every digital investment drives measurable outcomes", Data integrity and trust "protecting and governing the enterprise's most valuable asset", and AI enablement "helping the organization use AI responsibly, safely and at scale".

---

### CTO: Chief Technology Officer

**Differentiation from CIO:**

CTOs are very similar to CIOs, but CTOs make decisions for the overarching technology infrastructure that closely align with the organization's goals, while CIOs work alongside the organization's information technology staff members to perform everyday operations.

While the CIO typically oversees the organization's IT infrastructure and applications, the CTO primarily focuses on product development, research and development, and technology innovation.

**Evolving Responsibilities:**

The role of the CTO has been redefined to include strategic planning, spearheading digital transformation, managing cybersecurity, and driving innovation.

The CTO role is evolving faster than ever before, requiring the post-holder to connect and translate the creation and management of new products and services with more traditional enterprise IT and operational enablement activity.

---

### CISO: Chief Information Security Officer

**Authority and Scope:**

CISOs carry expanding responsibility as cybersecurity budgets rise, AI adoption spreads, and board expectations grow.

52% of organizations report that the CISO is responsible for OT (operational technology), up from 16% in 2022.

**Strategic Positioning:**

As organizations recognize the immense value and criticality of data and systems, cybersecurity has become intrinsically linked to business strategy, and CISOs are increasingly expected to play a central role in shaping business decisions, assessing and mitigating risks, and ensuring that security strategies align with overall business objectives.

## PAIN POINT ANALYSIS: TOP 5 RANKED

### #1: TALENT SHORTAGE & SKILLS GAP
**Intensity: CRITICAL**

The most often cited challenge by CIOs is a consistent and widening shortage of tech talent. Because it's impossible to meet their objectives without the right people to execute them, tech leaders are training internally as well as exploring non-traditional paths for new hires.

Talent shortages remain persistent, especially in security, data engineering, and AI-related roles.

**Manifestation:**

More than half the respondents said staffing and skills shortages "took time away from more strategic and innovation pursuits".

---

### #2: TECHNICAL DEBT ACCUMULATION
**Intensity: HIGH (But Deprioritized)**

Tech debt can take many forms — old applications, bloated code, and aging hardware among them, and 47% of those who expect to overspend blamed excessive tech debt, including old apps.

Technical debt can limit a company's ability to innovate as developers and other IT staff spend more time fixing a backlog of issues, which leads to less time for new technology development.

**The Paradox:**

Company boards and CEOs are putting pressure on CIOs to find innovative uses for AI, and the need for better cybersecurity never goes away. Therefore, always-lurking tech debt gets put on the backburner.

---

### #3: AI STRATEGY EXECUTION & ROI MEASUREMENT
**Intensity: HIGH (Growing)**

AI adoption is a priority for CIOs, with 92% expecting AI integration in their organizations by 2025. While the potential of AI is evident, many organizations struggle to realize measurable value.

While CEOs and business leaders are committed to GenAI adoption, CISOs and operational leaders lack the necessary guidance, clarity and resources to address security risks and infrastructure challenges associated with deployment.

**Pressure Point:**

The challenge is being able to clearly articulate and measure the value of these solutions, including how you're driving more revenue or lowering cost, with a knowledge gap we need to figure out.

### #4: CYBERSECURITY THREATS & REGULATORY COMPLEXITY
**Intensity: SUSTAINED**

84% say a successful breach is inevitable. Most CISOs are stretched thin, dealing with nonstop incidents, too many tools, and growing pressure from their boards. The pressures are so intense that many say they are burned out and thinking about walking away.

Cybersecurity and data privacy are top dispute concerns for businesses in 2025. Regulatory pressures, increasing frequency and sophistication of attacks and changing operational models are intensifying cybersecurity anxieties.

### #5: DEMONSTRATING IT VALUE & ROI
**Intensity: STRUCTURAL**

Just 26% of CIOs and technology executives rate themselves as "expert" in strategically managing IT budgets while balancing cost, value and risk at an enterprise level. But effective budget management is key to being a successful CIO.

## BUYING BEHAVIOR & VENDOR SELECTION

### Typical Vendor Engagement Process

Organizations should evaluate potential vendors based on factors such as capabilities, experience, pricing, and compliance with regulations and industry standards. Effective vendor evaluation and selection requires a deep understanding of the organization's IT needs, as well as the vendor landscape and market trends.

### Evaluation Criteria (Ranked by Importance)

CIOs define broad areas to assess, including customer satisfaction, product and vendor reliability, and how well the vendor helped achieve business goals.

CIOs never take on a new vendor without speaking to at least two or three companies that have used that vendor before, and it's important to ask the right questions.

### Validation & Due Diligence

CIO's approaches to evaluating SaaS vendors must change. CIOs still assess their vendors as though they are buying packaged on-premises software, looking at features and functions and then whittling those vendors down to three; they participate in a dog and pony show, have the team rate the spreadsheet, and then pick the winner, but that approach will not work in the SaaS world.

### Procurement Involvement

CIOs make executive decisions regarding matters such as the purchase of IT equipment from suppliers or the creation of new IT systems.

CIOs oversee the selection, acquisition, and management of technology vendors and partners. They liaise between the technology team and other departments and must understand the business objectives of these departments and align the technology strategy to support them.

## MESSAGING GUIDELINES FOR TECHNOLOGY LEADERSHIP

### Language Preferences

**DO:**
- Use business outcome language: revenue, cost reduction, efficiency, risk mitigation
- Reference specific metrics: uptime %, incident response time, project delivery rate
- Speak to pain (talent shortage, tech debt) before solutions
- Position advisor as extending CIO's bandwidth, not replacing judgment

**DON'T:**
- Use technical jargon when explaining business value
- Position as "vendor management software" or "procurement tool"
- Imply CIO needs more tools/platforms
- Assume CIO understands the full advisor market

### Tone & Register Adaptation

**For C-Suite (CEO/Board Context):**

CIOs spend as much time influencing behavior across the enterprise as they do managing technology. Use portfolio language: "governance," "orchestration," "strategic partnerships," "enterprise risk."

**For VP IT (Operational Context):**
CIOs are drowning in firefighting. Use relief language: "reclaim bandwidth," "automated oversight," "systematic tracking," "standardized process."

**For CISO (Collaborative Context):**

Measuring and communicating risk remains a top focus area for CISOs in 2025. Cyber Resilience superseded other focus areas to become the top CISO priority of 2025. Use security-specific language: "risk quantification," "compliance alignment," "vendor security posture."

### Technical Depth Calibration

The non-technical CIO has a huge advantage – if he or she can communicate well. "Business" is the language of businesspeople.

Avoid deep technical explanation unless explicitly requested. Focus on business translation instead.

## CROSS-VERTICAL INSIGHTS: ROLE VARIATION BY INDUSTRY

### Financial Services

For financial services, security and stability reign supreme. An interim CIO in this sector prioritizes bolstering cybersecurity defenses, ensuring regulatory compliance, and diligently managing risk. Modernizing legacy systems while preserving operational continuity is another key focus.

### Healthcare

Many organizations now view the CTO as a possible successor to the CIO, particularly as they become more involved in developing digital health strategies, and in fostering a culture of innovation.

### Manufacturing

Manufacturing interim CIOs concentrate on optimizing operations and boosting efficiency, which can involve deploying Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) solutions, refining supply chain management, and introducing automation. Working with legacy systems and sometimes resistant workforces requires strong change management skills.
